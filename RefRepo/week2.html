    • The theologians Rudolf Otto, in his phenomenological analysis of the religious experience, describes this breach of ordinary perception as the encourage with the numen praesense, the 'whole other', an object whose nature, trascending the known, breaks the boundaries of the conceivable and provokes in the individual or collective encountering it, unique types of emotions. Otto described this in paradoxical terms by employing the Latine mysterium tremendum and mysterium fascinans to characterize the numninous experience as a feeling-response bringing and holding together contrasting inner responses such as terror and ecstasy. The mysterium tremendum is depicted by Otto wiht dramatic emphasis, for exmple, as the 'awe-aweful' akin to 'religious dread', the horror of Pan 'with its queer perversion', the reaction to 'somthing uncanny', the weird, or the 'tremor, elicited by the encounter with what is regarded as unfamiliar or 'wholly other'. On the contrary, the mysterium fascinans is portrayed as the 'absolute fascination' that William James describes as the 'effect of some great orchestra, when all the separate notes have melted into one swelling harmony', the bliss, 'the wonderfulness', 'the rapture and the over-abounding'. feelings experienced by participating in something that lies beyond the singular and that the singular cannont fully possess. The two feelings described above coexist in Otto's conception of the religious experience and contribute to his illustration of a gentle tide that: 'may burst in sudden reuption up from the depths of the soul (...)   • Microinteractions: Chp. 1
    • "The design of your product is only as good as its smallest part." - p. 6
    • "In competitive markets, microinteractions are even more important. When there is feature parity, it is the experience using the product that increases adoption and brand loyalty." - p. 7
    • "Well-design microinteractions can scale well across platforms and to millions of users" - p. 8
    • "As DeRouchey says in "The History of the Button", 'The button meant of the first time the result of the human motion could be completely different from the motion [it creates] itself.' Action became abstract." - p. 12
    • Structure of a microinteraction
      • Trigger - initiates the MI
        • Understanding user need: objective, timing, frequency --> determines affordances, accessbility, and persistence of the trigger
      • Rules - how the MI works
      • Feedback - illuminates the rules
        • visual, aural, haptic (vibrations), animations
      • Loops and Modes - way to design and dissect the microinteraction
        • What happens to the MI over time?
    • 3 ways to incorporate MI into products
          Case-by-case
        • Make a list of all MI
        • Signature Moments: product differentiating MI --> creates customer loyalty and brand recognition
          Reduce complex apps into individual products built around one MI
        • MI as product with 1 job, reduced to its essence (e.g. Instagram)
        • Radical simplicity
          Complex digital products comprised of many MI
        • Details working in harmony to create the whole
    • Input methods
      • Physical: buttons, switches, keyboard, mouse
      • Touchscreens, senseors, voice, gesutral
  • The 5E Experience Design Model
    • "A designed experience flows lilke a river, pausing and at times rushing forwards, but always with rhythm and guiding purpose."
    • Create a High Concept
      • Create engagement, excitement, and curiosity for users
      • Empowers users to co-create/contribute to the experience
    • 5Es - Touchpoints
      • Excitement - awareness and attraction to the experience
      • Entry - crossing into one context to another
      • Engagement - keeping user attention
      • Exit - clear end of experience
      • Extension - takeaway for user
    • Journey Map
